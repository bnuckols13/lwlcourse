Freedom & Responsibility

"We are condemned to be free. Existence precedes essence." — Jean-Paul Sartre

Sartre's core claim is radical: there is no predetermined nature, no cosmic script, no authority telling you who to be. You exist first. Then you create yourself through choices. All of them.

Core message: There is no escape from freedom — only the illusion of escape. The eating disorder is one of those illusions. It offers the feeling of having no choice, which is temporarily less terrifying than having all the choices.

Bad Faith

Sartre's term for the move we make when we pretend we have no choice — when we become a "thing" rather than a free subject. Bad faith is not lying to others. It's lying to yourself about your own freedom.

Bad faith sounds like…

  • "I can't help it — that's just who I am"
  • "I don't have a choice in this"
  • "The disorder makes me do it"
  • "I'd change, but I can't"
  • "I'm not the kind of person who can…"

What bad faith is protecting against

  • The terror of being fully responsible for your own life
  • The grief of having made choices that hurt you
  • The exposure of admitting you want something different
  • The work of actually changing

Response-ability

Viktor Frankl, writing from inside a Nazi concentration camp, introduced the concept of response-ability: not the absence of difficult circumstances, but the ability to choose your response to them. Even in the worst conditions, that remains yours.

The distinction that matters

  • You did not choose your trauma, your neurology, your family, your disorder's origins
  • You did not choose to suffer
  • You do choose — every day — what you do next
  • Both are true. Neither cancels the other.
The responsibility piece can land as blame if it's not handled carefully. Make sure to hold both sides: the circumstances were real, the pain is real, AND the agency is real. The goal is not to shame anyone for past choices — it's to open the door to future ones. Watch for shutdown responses and name the distinction explicitly if needed.

Death & Finitude

"Death is the condition that makes it possible to live authentically." — Irvin Yalom

The existentialists were not morbid for thinking about death constantly. They thought about it because it's the one fact that makes everything else matter. Your time is finite. That means it has weight.

Core message: Awareness of your own finitude is not something to be managed or survived — it's a resource. When it's honestly reckoned with, it clarifies what matters and strips away the things that don't.

Being-Toward-Death

Heidegger argued that most of us live in a state of "falling" — absorbed in the everyday, distracted from the fact that we are going to die. This absorption is comfortable, but it costs us our authenticity.

When death becomes real to us — not abstract, but personally real — it can function as an "awakening experience." Suddenly, we see what we've been wasting time on.

What death-awareness can clarify

  • Which relationships are worth fighting for
  • Which fears are worth walking through
  • What you've been waiting for permission to want
  • How much of your life the disorder has already borrowed

Immortality Projects

Ernest Becker's insight in The Denial of Death: most human striving is an unconscious attempt to transcend mortality. We build "immortality projects" — systems that make us feel permanent, significant, invincible.

Eating disorders as immortality projects

  • Control over the body as control over vulnerability itself
  • Perfection as a defense against the randomness of being mortal
  • Shrinking as a way to feel somehow less subject to death
  • Rules as a substitute for the security that impermanence denies
The "immortality project" frame is often very resonant — particularly the idea of control as a defense against mortality. Some people in the room may be encountering this idea for the first time and find it clarifying. Give space for that. Some may push back — that's also worth exploring.

Things Worth Carrying Out

◆

◆

◆

◆
Wasting time is something you actually have the power to stop doing. Not all of it. Some of it.

Isolation & Connection

"The price we pay for consciousness is that we are forever alone. The most terrifying of existential concerns." — Irvin Yalom

Yalom identified three distinct types of isolation, each one real and each one calling for a different response.

Core message: The deep aloneness of the eating disorder is not just a symptom — it's an existential problem wearing a clinical costume. Understanding what kind of alone you are changes what you can do about it.

Type I — Interpersonal Isolation

  • Loneliness in the ordinary sense — lack of connection with others
  • The disorder creates and deepens this: secrecy, shame, rituals that isolate
  • Often the most visible type — the one people name when they say they're lonely

Type II — Intrapersonal Isolation

  • Disconnection from parts of yourself — emotions, desires, memories, body signals
  • The disorder accelerates this: numbness, dissociation, rules that override inner experience
  • Often invisible from the outside, but felt as an inner blankness or strangeness

Type III — Existential Isolation

  • The unbridgeable gap: no one can fully occupy your consciousness, or you theirs
  • This is not pathology — it's the human condition
  • The paradox: accepting existential isolation is the precondition for genuine connection
  • When you stop demanding to be fully known, you can actually be met

Meaning & Purpose

"He who has a why to live can bear almost any how." — Friedrich Nietzsche, cited by Frankl

Viktor Frankl developed logotherapy — the therapy of meaning — from his observation in the concentration camps that those with a reason to survive were more likely to survive. Not always. But significantly.

Core message: The eating disorder is a meaning system. It provides structure, purpose, identity, and direction. Recovery is not just removing that system — it's building a better one. That requires knowing where meaning actually comes from.

The Three Sources

Creative Values — What You Give

  • Meaning found in creating, contributing, building something
  • Work, art, caregiving, problem-solving — anything you make or offer
  • The disorder often attacks this: "nothing I make is good enough"

Experiential Values — What You Receive

  • Meaning found in beauty, love, truth, connection — what you encounter
  • A piece of music, a person, a landscape, a moment of understanding
  • The disorder often blocks this: rules and numbness close off receptivity

Attitudinal Values — How You Face What Can't Be Changed

  • Meaning found in the stance you take toward unavoidable suffering
  • The "last human freedom": no one can take your attitude from you
  • Frankl: "We can choose our response to our circumstances"
  • This is the source available even when the others aren't
Attitudinal values tend to land deepest for this population — the idea that even in circumstances they didn't choose, the stance they take is theirs. Connect this back to the Freedom session if you've done it. Also note: Frankl never romanticized suffering. Attitudinal meaning is not the same as "it was meant to be" or "everything happens for a reason." It's more honest than that.

◆

◆

◆

◆

I'm asking you to look at what's meaningful to you — not what should be meaningful, but what actually is. That's hard to see when the disorder has been filling that space. But it's there.

Thank you for being willing to look."

Authenticity & Self-Deception

"Authenticity is a relational good — it requires both the courage to be yourself and the courage to let others see you." — adapted from Charles Taylor

Authenticity is not about finding some perfect inner self. It's about the gap — the distance between what you actually experience and what you allow to show. Recovery often requires narrowing that gap, which is frightening.

Core message: The eating disorder creates and maintains the gap. It offers a performed identity that feels safer than the real one. Recovery is, in part, the willingness to let the real one be seen — even imperfectly.

Bad Faith Revisited

Sartre's bad faith appears in authenticity work as the tendency to become a "thing" — to define yourself by your role, your disorder, your diagnosis, rather than inhabiting your freedom moment by moment.

The false self protects against…

  • The terror of being known and still rejected
  • The grief of having hidden for so long
  • The vulnerability of wanting things openly
  • The responsibility that comes with being fully present

What authentic living asks for

  • Letting your actual emotional state be visible — not performing calm or OK
  • Saying what you need, even when it might not be given
  • Being in your body rather than managing it
  • Allowing relationships to include who you actually are
Authenticity work can easily become shaming — "you're not being your true self" can land as yet another way to be wrong. Emphasize that the gap is not a failure. It developed for reasons. The question is whether it still serves you, and whether it costs you more than it protects you. The work is not about exposure — it's about choosing which gaps to narrow.

Suffering & Growth

"Everything can be taken from a man but one thing: the last of the human freedoms — to choose one's attitude in any given set of circumstances." — Viktor Frankl

Frankl was not optimistic about suffering. He was honest about it. He did not claim it was good, or meant to be, or secretly a gift. He claimed something harder and more useful: that the stance you take toward it is yours.

Core message: Not all suffering is meaningful. Some of it is just suffering. But even in suffering you didn't choose and couldn't avoid, you retain the freedom to choose your attitude — and that choice is itself a source of meaning.

The Last Human Freedom

Meaningful vs. meaningless suffering

  • Meaningful suffering: unavoidable, faced with dignity — it shapes you toward something
  • Meaningless suffering: chosen, maintained, or created for its own sake — the disorder often produces this
  • The question is not "is this suffering" — it's "does this suffering have to be this way?"

Post-traumatic growth — honestly

  • Not "what doesn't kill me makes me stronger" — that romanticizes it
  • More accurately: "I was broken. Something new was forged in the breaking."
  • Growth is not the same as being glad it happened
  • You are allowed to hold both: this was terrible AND it made me who I am

The Phoenix archetype

  • Fire consumes what cannot survive fire
  • What remains is what was always real
  • Recovery is not returning to who you were before — it's becoming someone new
  • The person who recovers is always changed. That's not a loss.
Be careful with the "growth" framing — for some people, hearing that suffering produces growth can feel invalidating of their actual pain, or like pressure to find a silver lining. Keep the language honest: growth happens alongside grief, not instead of it. And not everyone experiences growth from trauma — that's real too, and should be acknowledged.

The Forge 15 min

Three stages of meaning-making from unavoidable suffering. Click each stage to open it.

◇
This exercise asks for something real. Take it at your own pace. You don't have to name anything you're not ready to name.
Stage I

Name the difficulty

What is the suffering — or the thing you've had to face — that you didn't choose?

This doesn't have to be the hardest thing you've ever been through. It can be something in your recovery, something in your history, something you're living right now. The question is: what have you been given that you did not ask for?
Stage II

Name the stance

What attitude have you taken — or could you take — toward it?

This is Frankl's attitudinal value. Not how you feel about it — how you hold it. Examples: enduring it with honesty. Continuing to seek help even when it's not working. Refusing to let it be the only story about you. Not pretending it's fine when it isn't. Staying in your body even when it's hard.
Stage III

Name what it shaped

Is there something in you — a capacity, a knowing, a way of being — that exists because of what you've been through?

You are not required to be grateful for your suffering. You are allowed to acknowledge what it made you capable of — without that being an endorsement of what caused it. The two things can be true at once.
